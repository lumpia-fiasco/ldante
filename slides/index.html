01 of 04 · Design Decision

Teamshares

The brief asked for a payroll view. I scoped it around one question: is this run normal?

The most useful conversation between an industry lead and a president wasn't "here's your payroll." It was "why is this run different from last time?" Period-over-period comparison, YTD context, and variance flags weren't in the original scope. I made the case for all three. They became the most-used features in production.

"Before this, I'd spend 45 minutes trying to figure out if a number was good or bad. Now I know in 30 seconds."— Industry Lead, post-launch feedback session

02 of 04 · Design Decision

Teamshares

Standard ATS logic assumes one candidate applies to one job. That's not how Teamshares works.

Teamshares places leaders — it doesn't fill requisitions. A single candidate might be right for three companies but only available for one right now. The "on deck" state — qualified, matched, waiting — doesn't exist in any standard ATS. I threw out the funnel model entirely and designed around the Teamshares-specific placement lifecycle instead.

On Decknew status with no standard ATS equivalent
N:Mcandidate-to-opening model vs. 1:1 in standard tools
10+leaders held on deck for future placements

Why the standard model didn't fit

Standard ATS

Screened for that job
Interviewed for that job
Offered or rejected
1 candidate → 1 opening → 1 outcome
vs.

Teamshares Model

Candidate enters the pool
Matched to potential openings
Placed — or held On Deck
On Deck → placed when match is ready
1 candidate → many futures → 1 placement at a time

Marketo / Adobe

The barrier wasn't missing features. It was fear of missing features.

Users weren't opposed to Sky. They assumed it didn't have what they needed — because nobody had shown them the map. We documented every Classic workflow, its Sky equivalent, and the honest gaps. Then we built the migration flow around that map. If your workflow was covered, you could move. If it wasn't, you stayed — and the gap went on the roadmap.

04 of 04 · Discovery

Meroxa

The users loved the product. The buyers didn't understand it.

Growth had stalled for two quarters. The team assumed the problem was onboarding — users weren't activating fast enough. I ran 12 churned-user interviews. One question broke everything open: "Who made the final call to stop using Meroxa?" The answer was never the data engineer. It was their manager. We were winning every technical evaluation and losing every budget conversation.

12churned users interviewed over 3 weeks
11/12cancellation decisions made by a manager, not the user
0of the budget decision-makers had seen a live demo

"My manager saw a CLI tool with a $2,000/month price tag and no dashboard. That was the end of the conversation."— Data Engineer, churned user interview

04 of 04 · Pivot

Meroxa

We stopped designing for the person using the product and started designing for the person buying it.

The data engineer still uses Meroxa — but the interface they see now surfaces the things a manager cares about: Is this pipeline healthy? What's the throughput? Who owns it? What would it cost to lose it? The UI became the proof of value, not the command line. The product didn't change. The audience for the product did.
